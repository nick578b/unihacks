Buddhism is the third-oldest still-widely-practiced religion, after Judaism and Hinduism. It was founded by Siddhartha Gautama (the Buddha) in India, and is still most prominent in South and Southeast Asia today. A majority of the population of the regions Bhutan, Myanmar, Cambodia, Tibet, Laos, Mongolia, Sri Lanka, and Thailand are Buddhist. Buddhists also live in China, Japan, Macau, Nepal, Vietnam, North Korea, etc, but they are not the majority religion. There are 488 million Buddhists in total worldwide, and 244 million Buddhists are Chinese.
The three major branches of Buddhism are Mahayana Buddhism, Theravada Buddhism, and Vajrayana. Mahayana Buddhism is the largest branch.
Buddhism focuses not a deity but on following a path to enlightenment, which is achieved through meditation and making moral and wise decisions. Buddhism is interpreted in many different ways by its different factions.
A Buddhist belief is that Gautama found enlightenment while meditating under a Bodhi tree. Followers of the religion believe the Dalai Lama is a reincarnation of a past lama that has agreed to be born again to help humanity. His image and story have evolved over the years.
